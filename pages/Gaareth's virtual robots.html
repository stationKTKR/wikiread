Gaareth's virtual robots are a series of autonomous computer programs created by computer scientist Milton Gaareth. These programs were designed with the intent to create "true digital life", and were capable of limited self-modification and self-replication. They would later be used to create some of the earliest web crawlers.

History

Milton "RAM" Gaareth was an American computer scientist. As an early cybersecurity expert, his work focused on finding and eliminating malware, particularly worms. Ironically, this work would inspire in him a fascination with digital organisms, and lead him to program his own computer worms, which Gaareth referred to as "virtual robots".[1] While his programs are technically classified as 'computer worms', he intended for them to be fully benign. Understanding that it might be possible for his robots to 'evolve' to be malignant, Gaareth kept them contained on servers disconnected from the Internet, so any unintended malware would only affect his own machines.

Gaareth would subject these relatively simple computer programs to 'chaos simulations', which he described as a "digital primordial soup".[2] Unlike other attempts at artificial life, Gaareth did not set out with an end result in mind, allowing the programs to evolve with minimal intervention. His reasoning was that digital life was more likely to be created "on accident" than "on purpose", and thereby tried to simulate as many "accidents" as possible by subjecting his robots to large amounts of data, mostly scraped from the Internet at random.

The fundamental problem Gaareth faced was determining what kind of 'metabolism' his digital lifeforms would have. Initially, Gaareth designed his robots to feed off data itself, but this proved troublesome when simulation devolved into a "cannibalistic orgy" of programs "devouring" each other.[3] While Gaareth made clear that he was not fundamentally opposed to his robots "eating" each other, reasoning that such behavior is common in existing lifeforms,[4], his simulations of these models could not survive beyond a handful of generations, never evolving to the extent that Gaareth had hoped. Other attempted sources of digital "energy", such as dead links and "clicks", would also prove unsustainable.

Near the end of his life, Gaareth maintained his belief in the emergence of "true digital life", even if he himself would not be one to create it. His virtual robot experiments continued, though Gaareth lamented that the project was becoming "literally, physically draining".[5]. A staunch opponent of generative AI and large language models, Gaareth felt that artificial intelligence research would be better served studying artificial life instead. In his final published academic paper, Gaareth wrote:

"There's already so little life in the Internet. We're either on the precipice of a Cambrian explosion or a digital extinction. I don't think I'll live to see it, but I pray it's the former."[6].

Years after his death, most of Gaareth's hardware was sold without being wiped, leading some of his programs to eventually fall into the hands of multiple tech companies. Likely without knowing the initial purpose of these programs, these companies would use his code as a basis for creating their own proprietary web crawlers[idiots], including Yahoo! Slurp, Msnbot, and Grub, which would later be acquired by Wikia.
