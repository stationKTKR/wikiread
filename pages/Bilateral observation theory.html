The bilateral observation theory, known more simply as "what you see sees you", is the assertion that any entity capable of observation must also be observable. Though originating in physics as a counterpart to the observer effect, this adage has been more commonly used in other disciplines, including artificial life and philosophy. When used by those studying alternative forms of life, this theory is interpreted as a cautionary reminder that, as we observe these unknown lifeforms, they are just as capable of observing us in turn.[lol]

Origin

While discussing the observer effect 1929, physicist Constance Plank coined the term "bilateral observation theory" to emphasize the necessity of considering the observer's role in any given experiment. As she explained, "Every experiment is limited by the tools used to measure its results. Just as observation alone can change the results of an experiment, observation itself is observable. Suppose you try to take a photograph of a mirror. Of course, the camera itself will appear in the photograph. Now suppose there is some other kind of 'mirror' we don't yet understand, some other entity capable of observation. There would be no possible way to observe this theoretical 'mirror' without it, in turn, observing us."
