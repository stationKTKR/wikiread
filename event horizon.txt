Milton Gaareth
Milton 'RAM' Gaareth was an American computer scientist. ran 'chaos simulations', creating simple bots (intelligent agents) that read through thousands of web pages,
  added bits of code to themselves, and propagated. these bots were given 'metabolisms' and 'life spans'
  distinguished from 'worms' and other types of malware in that 1 they did not insert themselves onto other machines, only propogating on gaareth's own hardware,
  and 2, spent 'energy' to crawl the internet, severely limiting their ability to affect network traffic in a significant way. 

  gaareth's greatest challenge in cosntructing this 'digital primordial soup' was defining what 'energy' would be for a digital organism. initially, 'information'
  was the 'food' of these digital beings, but, being made of information themselves, most of these simulations ended with a sort of 'cannibalistic orgy', ending
  in only a few generations at most. while gaareth was not fundamentally opposed to the idea of these organisms 'killing' or even 'eating' each other, he did not 
  see such an organism capable of surviving outside the 'warm little pond' of his texting environment. in order to sustain themselves, these organisms would need
  to be able to harvest their own 'energy'. gaareth tried several other forms of energy, such as 'clicks' and 'uncompresed images', but none of these trials
  resulted in 'true digital life'.

  despite failing to fulfill gaareth's dream of creating digital life, his bots, advanced for the time, were sold to burgeoning tech companies. and became some of
  the earliest web crawlers. in particular, Yahoo! Slurp, Msnbot, and Grub

described the internet as "a primordial soup of digital dna", theorizing that a form of intelligent, data-based lifeform was more likely to emerge naturally than   
  to be created by humans.
  "Our current artificial intelligence models, they just copy. And they're great at that, don't get me wrong. But it's a copy of something we already have.
  when I say 'digital life', i'm talking about something brand new. i don't think that's something we make on purpose. 
  
  But for something to be alive, it has to sustain itself
  We should ask ourselves, if a digital organism did exists--or, perhaps, already does exist--what would it eat? where would it live? and would we recognize it 
  if we saw it?"
